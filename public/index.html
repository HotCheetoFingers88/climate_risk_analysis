About the Sample Dataset
The built-in dataset covers 21 years (1990–2010) of simulated climate data for a hypothetical region. It demonstrates a clear trend: rising temperatures and declining precipitation are associated with increasing climate risk scores over time. It is intentionally well-behaved so the regression model fits cleanly — real-world data will be noisier.
Column Definitions
Column 1
Year

The calendar year of observation. Used as the x-axis in the timeline chart. Must be a 4-digit integer (e.g. 1990). This column is not used in the regression — it's just for labelling.

Column 2
Temperature (°C)

Annual average surface temperature in degrees Celsius. In the sample data this rises from ~14°C in 1990 to ~21°C in 2010, reflecting an accelerating warming trend. Used as a predictor (input) in the regression model.

Column 3
Precipitation (mm)

Total annual rainfall in millimetres. In the sample data this falls from 820 mm to 410 mm over the 21-year window. Lower precipitation = drier conditions = higher risk. Also used as a predictor in the regression.

Column 4 — Target
RiskScore

A composite climate risk index on an open-ended scale (sample data runs 32–91). Higher = more dangerous conditions. This is the value the model is trained to predict. In your own CSV this could represent any risk metric your data uses.

Output Metrics — what each number means
R² Score ≥0.85 good0.6–0.85 ok<0.6 weak
R² = 1 − (SS_res / SS_tot)
The fraction of variation in RiskScore that is explained by Temperature and Precipitation. An R² of 0.97 means the two variables account for 97% of why risk goes up or down. The remaining 3% is due to factors not in your data.
Sample data R² ≈ 0.997 — near-perfect because the dataset was synthetically generated to follow a clean linear trend.
MSE — Mean Squared Error
MSE = Σ(actual − predicted)² / n
The average squared gap between what the model predicted and what actually happened. Squaring penalises large errors more than small ones. A lower MSE is better, but the absolute value only makes sense compared across runs of the same dataset — 5.0 might be excellent for one scale and terrible for another.
If RiskScore is on a 0–100 scale and MSE is 0.8, predictions are off by roughly √0.8 ≈ 0.9 points on average.
Mean Risk Score
mean = Σ(x) / n
The arithmetic average RiskScore across all years in your dataset. A quick summary of the "typical" risk level. Sensitive to outliers — if one year is extremely high it will pull the mean up.
Median Risk Score
middle value when sorted
The middle value when all RiskScores are sorted. Exactly half the years fall above it, half below. More robust than the mean when anomalies are present. If mean and median differ a lot, your data is skewed.
Mean 62, Median 58 → a few high-risk years are pulling the average up.
σ — Standard Deviation
σ = √(Σ(x − mean)² / n)
How spread out the RiskScores are around the mean. A small σ means risk was fairly stable year-to-year; a large σ means high volatility. Shown under the Mean card as "σ = ...".
Δ Error (table column)
error = actual − predicted
The raw prediction error for each individual year. Positive = the model underestimated risk. Negative = overestimated. Large errors in specific years are worth investigating — they may reflect real events (drought, heatwave) not captured by temperature and precipitation alone.
Regression Model — how it works
Multiple Linear Regression
Fits a straight-line relationship between two input variables (Temperature and Precipitation) and one output (RiskScore). The model finds the three numbers b0, b1, b2 that minimise prediction error across your dataset. It assumes the relationship is linear and additive — each extra °C adds a fixed amount of risk regardless of the precipitation level.
b0 — Intercept
RiskScore when Temp=0 and Precip=0
The baseline RiskScore if both inputs were zero. Not usually meaningful on its own (0°C and 0mm precipitation is an extreme scenario) — it's just the mathematical anchor of the line. What matters is how b1 and b2 describe the change.
b1 — Temperature coefficient
risk change per 1°C increase
For every extra degree of temperature, RiskScore changes by b1 points. If b1 = 4.2, warming by 2°C is associated with +8.4 points of risk. A positive b1 means warmer = riskier, which is expected for most climate risk models.
b2 — Precipitation coefficient
risk change per 1mm increase
For every extra millimetre of precipitation, RiskScore changes by b2. This is usually negative — more rain = lower risk. If b2 = −0.05, a 100mm drop in annual rainfall adds 5 points of risk.
Correlation (Temp ↔ Risk)
r ∈ [−1, +1]
Pearson correlation between Temperature and RiskScore. +1 = perfectly increase together. −1 = one rises as the other falls. 0 = no linear relationship. Values above +0.7 or below −0.7 indicate a strong relationship worth including in the model.
Correlation (Precip ↔ Risk)
r ∈ [−1, +1]
Same measure between Precipitation and RiskScore. Typically negative for climate risk — drier years tend to have higher risk. If this correlation is near zero, precipitation may not be a useful predictor and b2 will be close to zero.
Anomaly Detection
Anomaly red highlight
|RiskScore − mean| > 2σ
A year is flagged as an anomaly if its RiskScore is more than 2 standard deviations from the average. Statistically, ~5% of data points in a normal distribution fall outside this range. Anomalies could represent real extreme events (heatwaves, droughts) or data entry errors — always worth checking.
Mean=60, σ=10 → anomaly if score <40 or >80.
Why 2σ?
Two standard deviations is a common statistical threshold — it flags roughly the most extreme 5% of values while ignoring normal year-to-year variation. A stricter threshold (3σ) would only flag truly extreme outliers. A looser one (1σ) would flag a third of all data. 2σ balances sensitivity and specificity for climate data.
How to read the charts
Timeline Chart (top)
Shows actual measured risk (solid teal line) and model-predicted risk (dashed yellow) over time. When the lines track closely, the model is accurate. When they diverge, something happened that year that temperature and precipitation don't explain. Red dots on the actual line indicate anomalies.
Scatter Plots (bottom two)
Each dot is one year. The x-axis is Temperature or Precipitation; the y-axis is RiskScore. Teal dots = actual risk, yellow dots = what the model predicted for that same x value. A clear diagonal band of dots indicates a strong linear relationship. Scattered or curved patterns suggest the linear model may not be the best fit.
